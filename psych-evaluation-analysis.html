Methodology & Reliability Concerns: Psychosocial Evaluation

Case: Hicks v. Vigneau (500-04-079464-237)

Evaluator: Frédéric Laterrière, Psychologist

Subject: Christopher Hicks

Materials Reviewed: Psycho-Social Evaluation Report & Session Audio Transcripts (02/09/2024)

Deontological Reference: Ordre des psychologues du Québec (OPQ) Code of Ethics

Part I: Identified Concerns — Process & Product

Note: The items below identify specific points where the evaluation process or the resulting report appear to depart from the OPQ’s requirements for clinical neutrality, evidentiary transparency, informed consent, and logical consistency. These concerns are raised for the court’s consideration and do not, by themselves, establish intentional misconduct.

Process: Language of Evaluation & Informed Consent The subject is a native English speaker. Psychometric instruments were administered in English, yet the evaluative interview was conducted in French. The report acknowledges this language difference but does not document what steps, if any, were taken to ensure the subject fully understood the questions posed and could articulate his responses with equivalent precision. Audio (02/09/24 09:06, L9-10): Evaluator indicates the custody process is in French and proceeds accordingly.

Report (L41-46): Notes that the subject “mostly expressed himself in English,” that the evaluator “checked his French comprehension,” and that the subject “accepted a report drafted in French.” 		OPQ Arts 5, 40 (Informed Consent): Where a language barrier exists, the evaluator bears the obligation to document that the subject understood the nature, purpose, and consequences of the process. Proceeding without such documentation raises concerns about whether consent was meaningfully informed. Critical “What steps did you take to document that Mr. Hicks fully understood and consented to proceeding in French for a high-stakes evaluative interview?”
Product: Selective Treatment of Contradictory Evidence Regarding the BIXI-related allegation, the report relies on unverified police tickets to conclude the subject has a tendency to distort the truth. However, the audio recording captures the subject explicitly denying the allegation and stating that the tickets do not correspond to his access schedule. This denial does not appear in the report. Report (L1367-1405): Characterizes subject’s account as “contradicted” by the tickets and treats this as evidence of deceit. 		OPQ Arts 7, 46 (Neutrality & Objectivity): An evaluator is expected to document and weigh both parties’ accounts rather than accepting one party’s evidence as dispositive while omitting the other’s contemporaneous response. Critical “Why does your report omit Mr. Hicks’ contemporaneous denial and his statement that the tickets did not match his access schedule?”
Product: Inconsistent Evidentiary Standard The report cautions the reader that “subjective perception is not objective reality.” However, several pages later, the report treats contested allegations of coercive control as “objectively verified” without identifying the independent evidence supporting that characterization. The basis for this transition from subjective claim to objective finding is not explained. Report (L105): “Subjective perception is not objective reality.”

Report (L1333-1410): States that “objectively collected data verifies the situation” and adopts the opposing party’s allegations of post-separation violence as findings. 		OPQ Art 46 (Objectivity): Where the evaluator articulates a distinction between subjective and objective evidence, the reader is entitled to see that distinction applied consistently throughout the report, with clinical assertions supported by identified source material. Critical “What independent evidence did you rely on to move from a contested allegation to a finding you describe as ‘objectively verified’?”
Process: Characterization of Recording Behaviour The report characterizes the subject’s practice of video recording interactions as “domination and coercive control” and recommends its cessation. However, during the evaluation session, the subject explained that he records interactions as a means of preserving evidence against what he describes as false allegations in a high-conflict setting. The report does not appear to consider this alternative explanation. Audio (02/09/24 13:32, L119-120, L163): Subject explains recording as a defensive documentation measure in response to false reports.

Report (L1354-1359): Characterizes recording as “coercive control” and recommends forced cessation. 		OPQ Art 48 (Diagnostic Rigour): Before assigning a clinical characterization to a behaviour, an evaluator is expected to consider and test reasonable alternative explanations. Research literature documents that targets of false allegations in high-conflict separations frequently record interactions for evidentiary purposes. High “What analysis did you perform to rule out a defensive evidence-preservation motive before characterizing the recordings as coercive control?”
Product: Observations Difficult to Reconcile with Recommendations The report’s own observational data describes the father-child relationship in positive terms. Despite these recorded observations, the report’s recommendations substantially restrict the father’s access. The reasoning connecting the positive observations to the restrictive recommendation is not articulated. Report (L198-201, 1039): “Observed interactions are fluid and spontaneous;” child is “joyful during pickups;” “no change in attitude seen between homes.”

Report (L1494-1500): Recommends restricted paternal access. 		OPQ Art 51 (Logical Consistency): Forensic recommendations should flow logically from the evaluator’s documented observations. Where the recommendation appears to contradict the observational data, the evaluator’s reasoning process warrants scrutiny. Critical “How do you reconcile your observations of fluid, spontaneous, and positive father-child interactions with a recommendation for substantially restricted paternal access?”

Part II: Evaluating the Trauma-Response Hypothesis

A Methodological Critique and Alternative-Explanation Analysis

Abstract

This analysis evaluates the reliability of the psychosocial report and the February 9, 2024 session transcripts by comparing two competing explanations for the subject’s subsequent legal disengagement: (1) generalized litigation stress or parental apathy (H0), and (2) a cue-specific response associated with the evaluation process itself (H3). Based on the timeline material and transcript excerpts identified here, the second explanation appears more consistent with the observed pattern, particularly the apparent temporal clustering of freeze-like responses around evaluator-related and institutional triggers. This analysis is not offered as a medical diagnosis. Rather, it is offered as a methodological critique of whether the evaluation process and resulting report satisfy basic requirements of neutrality, transparency, and evidentiary consistency.

I. Factual Chronology

The subject underwent a formal psychosocial evaluation across multiple dates, notably February 9, 2024 (09:06 and 13:32 sessions). Following these specific sessions, longitudinal timeline data and communication metadata show a marked change in the subject’s engagement with legal and institutional processes. This change—characterized by legal inactivity and difficulty executing procedural steps—appears to coincide temporally with the evaluation sessions rather than with the general arc of the litigation. The specificity of this temporal association warrants consideration when evaluating the report’s conclusions about the subject’s character and conduct.

II. Evaluator Background & Deontological Baseline

The evaluator, psychologist Frédéric Laterrière, authored a September 2022 peer-reviewed paper in the Revue québécoise de psychologie (Vol. 43, No. 2) on the topic of addressing parental defensiveness in custody evaluations. This publication suggests familiarity with techniques for managing resistance in forensic interview settings. The Ordre des psychologues du Québec (OPQ) Code of Ethics requires that psychological interventions—particularly those that may increase cognitive load or exploit linguistic vulnerability—be accompanied by informed consent and clinical transparency. Where such techniques are employed without documented consent, concerns about deontological compliance arise.

III. Process Audit: Transcript Review

A review of the raw February 9, 2024 audio transcripts identifies the following areas of concern:

  • Language of Interview: Despite the subject expressing himself primarily in English, the evaluative discussion was conducted in French (Transcripts: 24-02-09, 09:06, lines 9-10). Research on bilingual assessment indicates that conducting high-stakes evaluations in a non-dominant language can increase cognitive load and reduce the subject’s capacity to articulate nuanced responses. No documentation of the subject’s informed consent to this arrangement has been identified.
  • Handling of the BIXI Allegation: During the 13:32 session, the subject articulates a detailed response to the BIXI-related allegation (lines 33-43), including a specific claim that the tickets do not correspond to his custody access schedule. The manner in which this response was explored in the interview, and the fact that it was subsequently omitted from the report, raises questions about whether contradictory evidence was given adequate weight.
  • Consent Documentation: No documentation has been identified confirming that the evaluator obtained explicit, informed consent from the subject regarding the language in which the evaluation would be conducted, or regarding any specific interview techniques that may have been employed.

IV. Report Analysis: Internal Consistency

The following internal inconsistencies within the report are noted:

  • Inconsistent Epistemological Framework: The report states (line 105) that “subjective perception is not objective reality,” establishing a clear analytical standard. However, the report subsequently treats contested allegations of coercive control as “objectively verified” (lines 1333-1410) without identifying the independent evidence that supports this characterization. The basis for this transition is not explained in the report.
  • Omission of Recorded Statements: The report cites unverified police tickets to support a conclusion about the subject’s credibility (lines 1367-1405). The audio record captures the subject’s contemporaneous denial and his reference to schedule evidence that he says contradicts the tickets. This recorded response does not appear in the report.
  • Untested Alternative Explanation: The subject explained during the evaluation session (13:32, lines 119-120) that he records interactions to preserve evidence in what he describes as a high-conflict situation involving false allegations. The report characterizes this behaviour as “coercive control” (lines 1354-1359) without visibly testing the alternative hypothesis that it represents a defensive evidence-preservation strategy—a behaviour documented in research literature on high-conflict separations.
  • Observation-Recommendation Gap: The report documents that the subject’s interactions with the child are “fluid,” “spontaneous,” and “joyful,” with “no change in attitude” observed between homes (lines 198-201, 1039). The report then recommends substantially restricted paternal access (lines 1494-1500). The clinical reasoning connecting these positive observations to the restrictive recommendation is not articulated in the report.

V. Competing Explanations for Post-Evaluation Disengagement

If the subject’s post-evaluation legal disengagement were attributable to generalized litigation stress or apathy (H0), one would expect to see a diffuse pattern of withdrawal across multiple life domains, with gradual onset correlated to trial milestones. Instead, the available timeline data suggests a pattern that is more cue-specific: the subject’s difficulty appears concentrated in contexts associated with the evaluation and legal-institutional settings, rather than manifesting uniformly. This pattern is more consistent with a conditioned avoidance response linked to the evaluation experience (H3)—a phenomenon described in the institutional betrayal literature—than with generalized stress or disinterest. This observation is offered for consideration; a definitive determination would require formal clinical assessment.

VI. Conclusion

The materials cited here raise serious concerns regarding the reliability of the evaluation and the weight that should be given to its conclusions. The principal concerns are:

  • Possible informed-consent and comprehension issues arising from language use during the evaluation process;
  • Selective treatment of contradictory evidence, including the omission of the subject’s recorded denials and exculpatory statements;
  • Inconsistent application of the report’s own stated standard for distinguishing “subjective” from “objective” material;
  • Failure to visibly test alternative explanations for the subject’s conduct before assigning clinical characterizations; and
  • Recommendations that appear difficult to reconcile with the evaluator’s own recorded observations of the father-child relationship.

These concerns do not, by themselves, establish intentional misconduct. They do, however, justify close scrutiny of the evaluator’s methodology, source handling, verification steps, and reasoning process.
